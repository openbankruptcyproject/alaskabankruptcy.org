Chapter 13 Bankruptcy in Anchorage, Alaska

Chapter 13 lets you keep your property while repaying debts over 3-5 years. No means test required. Anchorage residents file in the District of Alaska at 605 W 4th Ave.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

How Chapter 13 Works

Chapter 13 is a reorganization bankruptcy. Instead of liquidating assets, you propose a repayment plan to pay back some or all of your debts over 3-5 years. You make one monthly payment to a Chapter 13 trustee, who distributes funds to your creditors according to the plan.

Key Advantages of Chapter 13

  • Keep all your property - No liquidation. You keep your home, car, and other assets.
  • Stop foreclosure - Catch up on missed mortgage payments over the life of your plan.
  • Protect co-signers - The co-debtor stay protects people who co-signed your consumer debts.
  • Cram down car loans - If your car loan is older than 910 days, you may be able to reduce the balance to the car's current value.
  • No means test - Unlike Chapter 7, there is no income limit for Chapter 13.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13

Chapter 7

  • Eliminates most unsecured debt
  • Takes 3-4 months
  • Requires passing means test
  • Non-exempt property may be sold
  • Cannot catch up on mortgage arrears

Chapter 13

  • Repay debts over 3-5 years
  • Keep all property
  • No means test required
  • Can catch up on mortgage and car payments
  • Protects co-signers on consumer debts

Plan Length and Payments

Your Chapter 13 plan length depends on your income relative to Alaska's median:

  • Below median income: Plan can be as short as 36 months (3 years).
  • Above median income: Plan must be 60 months (5 years).

Your monthly payment is determined by several factors: your disposable income, the value of non-exempt property, and the amount needed to pay priority debts (taxes, support) and catch up on secured debts (mortgage, car). Alaska's higher cost of living is reflected in the allowable expense calculations.

Chapter 13 in Alaska's Unique Environment

Alaska's geography and economy create unique considerations for Chapter 13 filers:

  • Seasonal income: Many Alaskans have seasonal employment (fishing, tourism, oil field work). Chapter 13 plans can account for irregular income with varying monthly payments.
  • PFD (Permanent Fund Dividend): Your annual Alaska PFD may be required to be turned over to the trustee during your Chapter 13 plan. The trustee's position on PFDs can vary.
  • Remote monitoring: The District of Alaska accommodates telephonic appearances for plan confirmation hearings and status conferences, important for filers in remote communities.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does Chapter 13 work in Anchorage?

You file a petition and propose a 3-5 year repayment plan. The automatic stay stops collections immediately. You make one monthly payment to a trustee who distributes funds to creditors. After completing the plan, remaining qualifying debts are discharged.

How long is a Chapter 13 plan?

If your income is below Alaska's median, your plan can be 3 years. If above, it must be 5 years. Alaska's higher medians mean more filers may qualify for the shorter plan. Compare Chapter 7 and Chapter 13.

Can Chapter 13 stop foreclosure?

Yes. Filing Chapter 13 immediately stops foreclosure through the automatic stay. You can catch up on missed mortgage payments over your 3-5 year plan while continuing current payments going forward.

Do I have to give up my PFD in Chapter 13?

Possibly. The Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend may be considered disposable income that should go to creditors during your Chapter 13 plan. The trustee assigned to your case will take a position on this. Discuss this with your attorney before filing.

What happens if I cannot keep up with payments?

If you fall behind, your case may be dismissed. However, you can request a plan modification if your circumstances change, or convert to Chapter 7 if you qualify. Contact your attorney as soon as you anticipate difficulty making payments.
